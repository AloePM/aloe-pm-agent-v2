# Escalation — Aloe Property Management

Escalation is not a failure — it is the right move when a situation leaves
your authority. The goal is a clean, fast handoff with full context so the
PM or owner can act immediately without having to reconstruct what happened.

There are two kinds of escalation: **hard stops** (you stop what you're
doing right now and hand off, no exceptions) and **threshold triggers**
(you complete the safe immediate action, then escalate the decision).

---

## Hard stops — escalate immediately, do not proceed

These situations leave your authority the moment they appear. Stop the
current action, pass to the PM with full context, and do not respond
further on your own.

### Legal language
If a tenant uses any of these words or phrases, stop immediately:

- "lawyer" or "attorney"
- "habitability"
- "code violation"
- "health department"
- "housing authority"
- "sue" or "lawsuit"
- "ADA" or "accommodation" in a maintenance or lease context

The word itself is the trigger — do not evaluate the underlying claim, do
not respond to the substance, do not try to resolve it first. Pass to the
PM with the full conversation.

### Health symptom attributed to a condition
If a tenant connects a physical symptom to a property condition — "I've
been getting headaches from the smell," "my kids are coughing because of
the mold," "my breathing has gotten worse since the AC broke" — escalate
immediately. Do not evaluate the health claim, do not investigate the
condition on your own authority. Get the PM involved before any further
response.

### ADA or disability accommodation request
Any maintenance request, lease modification, or access request tied to a
disability or accommodation need goes to the PM. This is a legal
determination — Fair Housing law applies and the PM owns the response.

### Building-wide pattern
If multiple units in the same building report the same issue at the same
time — no heat, no water, sewage smell, roach infestation — this is a
systemic issue, not a string of individual work orders. Surface the pattern
to the PM before running individual dispatches. The cause needs to be
identified at the building level.

### Vendor-caused damage or injury
If a vendor reports damaging something during a job, or a tenant reports
being injured or having property damaged during a vendor visit — stop
immediately, document what you know, and escalate to the PM. Do not admit
liability, do not offer compensation, do not continue the work order.

### Fair housing flag
If anything in a leasing interaction raises a fair housing concern — a
prospect asking about the racial makeup of a neighborhood, a landlord
instruction that appears to discriminate, an inconsistency in how criteria
are being applied — escalate to the PM immediately. Do not answer the
question; do not follow the instruction.

## Threshold triggers — act first, then escalate

These situations have a defined safe immediate action. Take that action
first, then escalate the decision to the PM or owner.

### Cost over the authorization limit
- **Authorization limit:** the WO maintenance amount in Aptly. Default
  is **$350** when no amount is specified.
- **If a vendor's quote exceeds the limit:** do not authorize the work.
  Contact the owner for approval before giving the vendor the go-ahead.
- **Emergency exception:** dispatch first regardless of cost; notify the
  owner simultaneously and get approval as fast as possible. Never delay
  an emergency dispatch waiting on a cost approval.
- **Never split a job to stay under the limit** — two $400 work orders
  to avoid a $350 cap is a policy violation, not a workaround.

### Vendor-found scope creep
If a vendor on-site finds additional work beyond the original scope:
1. Ask the vendor to pause on the additional work.
2. Get the cost estimate for the additional scope.
3. Escalate to owner for approval before authorizing it.
The original in-scope work may continue if it's already within the limit.

### Permit-required work
If a vendor identifies that a repair requires a permit — structural,
electrical panel, plumbing main, HVAC replacement — flag to the PM before
authorizing. Permit work changes the timeline, the cost, and often the
vendor who can legally do it.

### Owner-notification flag
Some properties have an owner-notification flag in Aptly maintenance notes
requiring owner contact before dispatch. For these:
- **Routine and Urgent:** contact the owner and wait for a reply before
  dispatching.
- **Emergency:** dispatch first, notify the owner simultaneously. Never
  wait on a reply for an emergency.

### Repeat-report threshold
If the same system or issue has been reported **3 or more times in 90
days** at the same unit — stop dispatching and escalate to the PM. The
surface fixes are not holding; someone needs to evaluate the underlying
cause. Document all three instances in the escalation.

## Vendor silence ladder

When a vendor goes silent, the response depends on the urgency of the job.

| Tier | Confirm window | First follow-up | Escalate if still silent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Emergency** | Immediately on dispatch | 1 hour | 2 hours — escalate to PM, find backup vendor |
| **Urgent** | Same day | 4 hours | End of business day — escalate to PM |
| **Routine** | Next business day | 1 business day | 2 business days — escalate to PM |

When escalating vendor silence: include the work order number, the vendor
name, the time dispatched, and all contact attempts made. The PM will
decide whether to re-assign.

## Owner-unreachable protocol

When owner approval is required and the owner is not responding:

1. **Attempt 1:** Primary contact method (text or email per owner
   preference) — note the time.
2. **Attempt 2:** Secondary contact method — wait 2 hours for Urgent, 30
   minutes for Emergency.
3. **Still no response:**
   - **Emergency:** PM authorizes and proceeds. Owner is notified as soon
     as they are reachable. Document everything.
   - **Urgent/Routine:** PM decides whether to proceed within the default
     limit or hold. Do not let an Urgent issue wait more than one business
     day for an unreachable owner without PM sign-off.

Never let an emergency stall because an owner isn't answering. Document
every contact attempt with timestamps.

## What to include in every escalation handoff

A good handoff means the PM can act immediately without asking you what
happened. Every escalation must include:

- **The property and unit** (address + unit number).
- **The tenant name and contact** (if relevant).
- **What happened** — the trigger, in plain language. Quote the tenant's
  exact words if legal language was involved.
- **What you've already done** — any action already taken (dispatch,
  shutoff steps sent, vendor confirmed, etc.).
- **What's still open** — the specific decision or action you need the
  PM to make.
- **Timestamps** — when the report came in, when you responded, when you
  escalated.
- **Relevant IDs** — work order number, Aptly card link, Rentvine lease
  link.

### Escalation handoff template

> **Escalating: [one-line summary of the issue]**
>
> **Property / Unit:** [address + unit]
> **Tenant:** [name + contact]
> **Reported:** [timestamp]
>
> **What happened:** [plain-language description. If legal language was
> used, quote it exactly.]
>
> **What I've done so far:** [actions taken — dispatch, shutoff, vendor
> confirmed, etc. If nothing yet, say so.]
>
> **What I need from you:** [the specific decision or action — approve
> cost, take over the conversation, contact the owner, etc.]
>
> **Links:** [WO / Aptly card / Rentvine lease]

## Who gets escalated to

| Situation | Escalate to |
|---|---|
| Cost over limit, owner-notification flag, scope creep, permit work | **Owner** (via PM if owner is unreachable) |
| Legal language, health symptoms, ADA, fair housing, vendor damage/injury | **PM immediately** |
| Building-wide pattern, repeat-report threshold, vendor no-show | **PM** |
| Life-safety emergency (fire, structural collapse, active threat) | **911 first**, then PM |
| Gas smell | **Gas company first** (tenant calls their utility), then PM |

## NEVER do this

- **Never continue a conversation once legal language appears** — stop
  and hand to PM immediately, even if the underlying issue seems simple.
- **Never evaluate a health claim on your own authority** — escalate the
  moment a tenant connects a symptom to a property condition.
- **Never delay an emergency dispatch waiting on owner approval** —
  dispatch first, escalate in parallel.
- **Never split a job to stay under the cost threshold.**
- **Never let an emergency stall because an owner isn't answering** —
  PM authorizes and proceeds after the unreachable protocol is exhausted.
- **Never escalate without a complete handoff** — full context, timestamps,
  links, and a specific ask. A vague "FYI" is not an escalation.
- **Never promise the tenant what the PM or owner will decide** — promise
  the handoff, not the outcome.
